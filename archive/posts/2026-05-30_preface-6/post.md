And if you, like me, attended at least one of the twenty shows on the Land of Hope and Dreams American Tour, we sang something.

This book exists because I wanted to *say* something.

If you bought this book, a donation equal to fifteen percent of the cover price has been made to the Northwest Immigrant Rights Project, because I wanted to *do* something.

[Watch on Youtube: Watch Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vs3_midSvqs)

And if your name appears below, that means you did something too.

Bruce didn’t have to tour in order to speak out. He’s been doing that all along.

He’s touring because he knows there is power in community, and strength in numbers. The words and actions of any one of us–even someone with a following as large as Springsteen’s–are just that: our own.

But when we hear our voices as part of a choir and our words as part of a chorus… when we know our individual deeds and local actions are part of an international campaign of resistance and kindness… we are strong. Powerful. Gravitational.

We create a butterfly effect that influences and inspires our friends, family, and neighbors to say, sing, and do something, too. We make it safe for others to come out. We may not feel it directly, but we feel it innately.

While researching for the “American Skin (41 Shots)” essay I wrote for this volume, I came across a *New York Post* interview with Kadiatou Diallo. It was published on June 13, 2000, the day after Bruce Springsteen performed the song he had written in her son’s memory for a sold out Madison Square Garden crowd that included Diallo and her husband.

In the interview, Diallo recounted the backstage meeting they had with Springsteen before the show. She thanked him for keeping her son’s memory alive, to which Bruce reportedly responded, “I did what I could do.”

I was struck by the simplicity, the humility, and most of all the *intentionality* of those words.

*I did what I could do*.

This book is what I could do. Thank you for doing what you can do.

This book is for you.
