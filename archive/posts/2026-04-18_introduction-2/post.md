Whenever I completed an entire album’s worth of *Roll of the Dice* articles, I’d celebrate by writing an *Album Companion* essay that served both as backstory and review, summing up the underlying themes of the album.

As many readers noticed (and pointed out to me via email), I never published one for *Born in the U.S.A.*

It wasn’t an oversight.

I didn’t know how to approach it. In complete candor, I still don’t.

For one thing, it’s impossible to tell the origin story of *Born in the U.S.A.* without also relating the story of *Nebraska*. Both albums were sourced from the same group of songs, and as we’ve seen in recent years with the release of *L.A. Garage Sessions ’83* and *Electric Nebraska*, it can be difficult to define the boundaries around Bruce’s 1982-83 songwriting and recording.

I’m not just speaking with the benefit of hindsight. Even at the time, Bruce Springsteen wasn’t always clear about what album he was recording on any given day.

Bruce has often expressed ambivalence about his all-time best-selling album. In a 2025 interview with *Rolling Stone*, he referred to it as “a record I put out… the record I made, not necessarily the record that I was interested in making.”

Unlike the vast majority of his catalog, there’s no theme that unites the album. *Born in the U.S.A.* is arguably the least cohesive album in his catalog.

That’s even reflected in the title. Almost all of Bruce’s albums carry a title that encapsulates or symbolizes the album. *Greetings from Asbury Park, N.J.* announced his arrival; *The River* captures the ebb and flow of a live Springsteen concert; *Tunnel of Love* introduces a collection of love songs both bright and dark; *Wrecking Ball* symbolizes the economic destruction chronicled by the album’s tracks.

*Born in the U.S.A.,* by contrast, says nothing about the album’s contents. I would argue that despite the artist’s laments of being misunderstood, it’s a title chosen deliberately to package Springsteen as an all-American pop superstar, which is exactly what he became upon the album’s arrival in record stores and radio stations.

While the title track is certainly one of the most famously misunderstood songs of all times, I think Springsteen (or at least his management) bears some responsibility for that.

If there’s any way to sum up the songs on the album, it would be through its irony: Never before or again would the E Street Band sound as muscular, even as they breathe life into material written by a songwriter at the height of fragility.

This is an album that’s simultaneously frat party and pity party, swinging like a pendulum between protest and complicity.

Somehow it broke through, seized hold of the zeitgeist, and refused to let go for the astounding *eighty-four weeks* it spent in the Billboard Top Ten list. (It took more than another year for it drop out of the Top 200.)

Its lead single gave Bruce the closest thing he’d ever have to #1 song until “Streets of Minneapolis” more than four decades later, and it would go on to spawn another half-dozen Top Ten hits.

The videos would become iconic, the the concert tour a can’t-miss experience even for casual fans.

And yet to many fans (this one included), it’s his most perplexing album: a collection of undeniably great songs that never quite gel as an album, even for the artist himself.

So that’s why I never wrote an album companion essay for *Born in the U.S.A.,* and why this introduction is sort of an anti-introduction.

This is an album where the sum of its parts is greater than the whole, and the best way to appreciate the album is to simply let the songs speak for themselves.

So let’s listen to them.
