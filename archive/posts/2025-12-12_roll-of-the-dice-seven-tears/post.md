Here's another diamond in the rough from Bruce's home studio recording sessions in early 1983: a short, tight lament called "Seven Tears."

[Watch on Youtube: Bruce Springsteen - Seven Tears (Official Audio)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UHoe0T9Rxd0)

In Bruce's typical "I had a job, I had a girl" fashion (in fact, the first two verses foreshadow two songs that would end up on his next album–"[Downbound Train](../2019-01-22_roll-of-the-dice-downbound-train/post.md)," and the title track, "[Born in the U.S.A.](../2019-09-21_roll-of-the-dice-born-in-the-u-s-a/post.md)"), Bruce neatly and efficiently lays out the story of a man who had it all and lost it all–and now he memorializes his seven years of happiness with a facial tattoo of seven tears as a mark of his everlasting sadness.

> *I had a wife, I had a child*<br>
> *I had a home up on a hill*<br>
> *Had a good life for seven years*<br>
> *Now all I've got is seven tears*<br>

> *Went down to see my gypsy man*<br>
> *He said, "Now son, I understand"*<br>
> *When I said, "I want one for every year"*<br>
> *He tattooed on my face these seven tears*<br>

> *Oh seven tears, oh seven tears*<br>
> *I want one for every happy year*<br>
> *My baby's gone, she's gone, gone, gone*<br>
> *And I'll cry forever ever on*<br>

> *When I walk out into the lights of town*<br>
> *People see me coming and they gather round*<br>
> *They wanna know how I ended up here*<br>
> *With this tattoo on my face of seven tears*<br>

> *Oh seven tears, oh seven tears*<br>
> *I want one for every happy year*<br>
> *My baby's gone, she's gone, gone, gone*<br>
> *And I'll cry forever ever on*<br>

By showing his sorrow on the outside, he prompts the people he encounters to ask him what's behind it. And that's precisely by design: our narrator's punishment is his penitence. Unable to forgive himself or move on, he ensures that he's forced to relive his love and retell his loss every day.

Musically, Bruce slowed and recycled the melody he used for "Little Girl Like You" a few weeks earlier. Lyrically, however, "Seven Tears" is a masterful example of economy in songwriting. Both "Little Girl Like You" and "Seven Tears" are about ninety seconds long, but whereas the former is a lazy trifle, the latter is a complete tale with an emotional wallop.

It deserves its official release on *L.A. Garage Sessions '83.*

**Seven Tears**<br>
**Recorded** February 15, 1983<br>
***Released:** L.A. Garage Sessions '83* (2025), *Lost and Found* (2025)<br>
