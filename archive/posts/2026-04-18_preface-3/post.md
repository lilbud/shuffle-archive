I thought this would be one of the easiest *Roll of the Dice* installments.

I was certain of it, in fact.

I’d long ago completed all of the essays for the entire album, including B-sides and outtakes, so I’d have no new writing to do.

None of the existing essays demanded polish–even the earliest ones held up to my critical eye as I read them years later. In fact, I think several of them reflect some of my best work: the local history lesson in “My Hometown” for example, the cinematic analysis of “Downbound Train”, the personal resonance of “No Surrender, or the storytelling in “Glory Days.”

Yep, I was pretty sure this book was essentially going to give me a month off, which I’d use to work on the *No Kings* bonus book.

Boy, was I wrong.

That realization started dawning on me almost immediately–literally from the first words of the first essay. I’d written “Born in the U.S.A.” as the second installment of a three-part series, sandwiched between its ancestor “Vietnam” and its sibling “Shut Out the Light,” (The opening sentence reflects it, as you’ll see shortly.)

When sequenced as the opening chapter in the book, it made me feel like I was starting in the middle of the story. I could solve that by including the entire three-track sequence, but that would break the album flow, as well as insert an outtake and a B-side into the mix.

I solved by making “Vietnam” and “Shut Out the Light” bookends to the main album. You’ll see the former titled as a prologue and the latter as an epilogue. It’s a break from how I’ve structured these *Roll of the Dice* volumes, but it seemed a good compromise that allowed me to preserve the album sequencing while still providing the additional essays in the order they were meant to be read.

But that opened a new can of worms: the outtakes. *Born in the U.S.A.* had dozens of them–enough to fill a book as large as the *Kingdom of Days* volume if I included them all. But almost all of them have been officially released by now, either on *L.A. Garage Sessions ’83* (which has its own book), *Tracks* (coming next year), *The Essential Bruce Springsteen* bonus disc, or other miscellaneous releases.

I decided to only include outtakes that wouldn’t show up in another volume, which left only a few, like “Murder Incorporated” or “None But the Brave.” Even then, there were edge cases. Should I include “Stop the War,” a full band rehearsal from early 1982? What about “Protection,” recorded by Bruce and the band but given away to Donna Summer?

I opted yes for both, but said no to many others. They’ll all show up sooner or later–in *Nebraska, Tracks,* or a miscellany book on the ones he gave away or never brought to completion.

Even the stats proved challenging. This is the first book I’ve prepared with a tour in progress, and several of these album tracks show up nightly. You’ll know when this book was put to bed by the “Last performed” dates that I know will be out of date before I even receive my own copy.

And then there are the covers. This album has more covers than any other Springsteen album, and the sheer number of *Cover Me* articles I’d written on them would fill a couple of volumes on their own. I had literally dozens of cover spotlights for both “I’m on Fire” and “Dancing in the Dark,” so many that towards the end of the blog I’d started to feature five or six at a time in *Cover Me Round-Up* posts.

I knew I couldn’t include them all, but to even include a representative sampling threatened to make the book a *Cover Me* volume with a handful of *Roll of the Dice* bonus tracks.

In the end, I made some painful inclusion and exclusion decisions and cobbled together some new round-up posts to keep the page count manageable. Even so, this is now easily the largest *Roll of the Dice* book to date.

What I thought would be a simple and straightforward project instead turned out to require a host of judgment calls. Ultimately, though, I’m happy with the result, and I hope you are too.

*Born in the U.S.A.* is one of Springsteen’s most important records, and I wanted this volume to be a comprehensive examination and celebration of it.

I hope I’ve succeeded.
