You knew this was coming, didn't you?

Over the blog's lifespan, I waxed poetic over the Bruce Springsteen Band era way too many times to not devote an entire volume to it.

(Is this the first book ever published that solely examines the Bruce Springsteen's Band's body of work? I think it might be.)

And you knew it was gonna come in the summer, too. That's when I used to break out my BSB bootlegs, crank up the volume, and listen outside. Something about that glorious, horn-fueled, gospel singer-backed, jazzy/poppy sound always felt radiant to me–like it was too bright to contain indoors.

This is the first *Roll of the Dice* book not based on a released album, so I got to play with chapter sequencing in a way I don't usually get to do. That meant I had to both re-listen to every song (a consistently delightful burden), as well as re-read every essay (a sometimes satisfying experience and other times less so).

A few of the entries in this book were written during the earliest days of the blog, when I was still growing not just as a writer, but more importantly as a listener. I think I gave myself a pass in a couple of them.

However, there are other entries s I'm quite proud of, including "You Mean So Much to Me" and "Down by the Riverside," which bookend the volume.

Writing "Down by the Riverside" in particular was my most memorable experience on the blog. It brought to my readers and to the community a never-before-heard Bruce Springsteen song. And not just any song, but an intensely intimate. autobiographical song about an immense loss that Bruce wrote about decades later but into which we'd never had a contemporaneous glimpse.

I never derived any kind of financial benefit from writing the blog, but one very cool nonfinancial by-product was that as my reputation as a research historian and archivist grew, rare and unknown recordings began to find their way to me. They were offered to me in secrecy and trust by readers both inside and outside certain circles, to hold in a sort of escrow lest something happen to the folks holding onto the originals.

I always, *always* kept my word to never publish any of them without permission, but I also never stopped trying to secure said permission. Sometimes I was successful. Mostly, I wasn't. (Sitting on *Electric Nebraska* for years was one of the hardest things I ever had to do.)

I only ever actively sought out one recording, the 1972 Bruce Springsteen Band soundbaord recording that contained "Down By the Riverside." It was also the only uncirculating recording I ever purchased, and for a pretty penny, too.

I agreed to the amount because I wasn't just buying purchasing the recording–I'd also insisted as a condition that I be permitted to release "Down by the Riverside" to the community. Which I did, via this blog post.

If you read the post, you'll see I trace the origins of the recording all the way to its acquisition by a private collector via auction, without any explanation of how the recording then made it to my blog. Now you know a little more of the story. The rest will stay with me.

"You Don't Leave Me No Choice" is probably my other favorite essay in this collection. I made Bruce's backup singers my point-of-view characters, leading me down a fascinating rabbit-hole of interviews and research.

There's also one brand-new entry written for this book, the only (circulating) Bruce Springsteen Band song that I never got around to writing about: "Come On Billy, Break Out the Wine." The song itself is a trifle, but listening closely to it for the first time in a long time made me laugh at little elements that offer a sneak peek at Springsteen near-future.

If you're reading these words, it means you're interested enough in Springsteen's "prehistoric" era to learn more about his formative years and transitional bands. I encourage you to scan the QR codes and listen to the audio–*really* listen–and enjoy the music to the fullest.

Preferably outside on a warm, sunny day.
