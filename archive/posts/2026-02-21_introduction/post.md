In the long arc of Bruce Springsteen's career, 1992 is remembered as the year when the E Street Band was officially in the rearview mirror, the year of the double-album gamble, and the year both critics and fans sharpened their knives for the first time.

Neither *Human Touch* nor *Lucky Town* were well-received at the time. As Bruce would put it many years later, *Human Touch* in particular was “star-challenged.” And for good reason: the album sounds like an artist trying to find his footing in a high-tech studio. It’s overly slick, over-produced, and more dated-sounding than anything else in Bruce’s catalog.

*Lucky Town,* however, is a different story. It suffered from mere proximity to its fraternal twin; had it been released a year later, it might have earned the reception it deserved.

Bruce spent years agonizing over *Human Touch*, layering tracks with session pros and polishing until all of his trademark grit was gone. Then, in a burst of inspiration, he recorded *Lucky Town* in just a few months.

He didn’t just record an album, however–-he found his voice.

On *Lucky Town,* Springsteen traded in his anger for newfound domestic bliss. That part was new. But when we listen closely to the the album, it still sounds like Bruce: defiant and courageous, now laced with the vulnerability that first appeared in *Tunnel of Love* and even a trace of self-mockery.

Sonically, the gloss of *Human Touch* is gone, replaced by an almost garage-band aesthetic. Except for drums and keyboards (Roy Bittan is the only E Street Band instrumentalist on the album), Bruce plays every instrument himself.

This book is a deep dive into an album about the courage to be happy, with essays drawn almost entirely from my *E Street Shuffle* blog over the years. (“Leap of Faith” is brand new and exclusive to this book.) We’ll take a close listen to each and every track, and with luck we’ll come away with a greater respect for an often overlooked and under-appreciated record.

Let’s go down to *Lucky Town*.
